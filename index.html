The thesis

Half the state went on curfew.
We fished anyway.

A heat dome parked over Montana the week of July 8th and started breaking records that had stood since the 1920s — Forsyth hit a provisional 118°F, a would-be all-time state record. By the time we landed, Fish, Wildlife & Parks was rolling out hoot-owl closures across the southwest: the Big Hole, the Madison, the Jefferson, the Ruby, the Beaverhead — all of them off-limits after 2 p.m.

The Yellowstone — the big freestone next door to DePuy's — never made the list. But it was riding the edge: by our last fishing day the Livingston gauge touched 72.7°F, one-third of a degree from the 73° trigger, its flow sagging all week. However, thanks to Sparky's prescience, we had booked the two waters months earlier that could not have cared less. "I think we probably got the best of it, fishing tailwaters and spring creeks."

RM 0.0 · Friday, July 10

No love from Buzz

EWR and BOS to Bozeman, bags into the rental, straight to Paradise Valley. First stop: the little fly shop on DePuy's — Spring Creek Specialists, Buzz Basini's one-man operation next to Betty's Riffle, the kind of place with a hammock outside and better hatch intel than any website. Closed. "We drove to the ranch to try to get flies from Buzz, but he wasn't there."

So we bombed east to the West Boulder, above the falls, and salvaged the evening. One fish came to hand, and it was not Luyen's. At least Luyen got to take a bunch of mosquito bites back to the Airbnb as West Boulder souvenirs.

DePuy's Spring Creek · Saturday, July 11

Hot fishing on the DePuy

Sixteen rods a day on three miles of gin-clear spring creek, $150 at the white house, fish until dark. PMDs coming off like weather. The unlock to fooling the fish was a size 20 rusty spinner. As our buddy Jamie from Yellowstone Angler would tell us later: "One in seven spinners on the DePuy has a red body. For whatever reason, they seem to key on the rusty spinner."

"Three fish on the DePuy's in a day is a good day." — A FELLOW ANGLER WE MET STREAMSIDE THAT MORNING. DISPROVED BY THE END OF THE DAY.
Saturday, evening · the lower creek, through the cow fields

The evening hatch

We came off the water into 107° air and retreated to the Airbnb to rehydrate and siesta. By 5:30 we were back on the creek — this time the lower water, where it meanders through the cow fields.

Thorne chose the downstream beat. Luyen found a honey hole and proceeded to pick off eight browns — smaller fish, but steady — through the heavy evening hatch, every one of them on the rusty spinner. Thorne managed a couple as well. No photographic evidence of the fish exists — only the scene of the crime, and a witness.

“I found a pod of fish. I was on a lot of fish.”

Sunday was the lower water — riffle-and-flat cottonwood country down toward the Yellowstone confluence, where the bird-sanctuary ponds hold trumpeter swans and the browns tuck under weed rafts. By afternoon the PMD duns were stacked on every blade of grass on the bank.

Monday, July 13 · Day I on the Mo

Clouds of Tricos

"Like I've never ever seen Tricos like that." On the water below the dam by a little after seven, boats everywhere, and the air itself full of spinners catching the light like snow that refused to land.

The catching was harder than the looking. The fish sat in small, selective pods — risers you had to hunt, cross to, and beg. Thorne got one almost immediately on a tiny Trico on the far bank; then the river made everyone work. A sighted fish at the big island refused everything both of us threw — a true PhD candidate. The duckweed scum-suckers were nearly as educated: Thorne hooked one from the boat and it ran straight down into the leaves.

"Long line release."
— T. SPARKMAN, ON THE SCUM-SUCKER THAT RAN INTO THE DUCKWEED
THE FISH DISAGREES WITH THIS CHARACTERIZATION.
Tuesday, July 14 · Day II on the Mo

No love, then the beaver dam

Back to Monday's best-looking water — the prime-timey bank — and it gave "no love whatsoever." One on a long cast, one hooked and lost on the dropper. Then a long quiet drift, a two-island riffle where the bobber crowd was hooking up, and finally the payoff: a flat below a beaver dam, gray light, and sipping fish on dries to end the trip.

The wind made it a graduate seminar. Quartering-left casts, wind shoving twelve-foot leaders right. "I definitely learned something on that spot about throwing three feet of 5X against the wind. It just doesn't work."
